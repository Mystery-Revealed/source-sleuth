# "Source Sleuth" — Build Specification
### Unit 8 Game · Social Studies Skills · *Common Build Standards apply (Pattern A — Engine Game)*

## 1. At a Glance
| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| **TEKS** | 7.20A (primary vs. secondary; valid sources), 7.20D (bias and point of view), 7.20F (credibility), 7.20E (claim + evidence) |
| **Role** | A student historian solving "The Case of the Gonzales Cannon" (everyone plays the sleuth — class-wide group) |
| **Type** | Solo mystery — 6 evidence phases × 2 decisions = **12 graded actions**; an evidence board fills as the case builds |
| **Adapter** | `sourceSleuth.js` · meters `{ evidence, credibility, case }` start 50 |

**Pitch:** Your museum found a battered old cannon in a barn, tagged "the Gonzales gun?" — and it's your job to find out. Six sources, six tests, one verdict: is this THE "Come and Take It" cannon? Real historians work exactly this way. Time to sleuth.

**Teaching idea:** every 7.20 skill, played as detection. Each phase hands the student one source about the cannon; decision 1 **classifies and reads** it (primary or secondary? who made it, when, why?), decision 2 **weighs** it (bias? corroboration? does it move the case?). The finale asks for a claim supported by evidence (7.20E) — and the honest answer is a historian's answer: *probably, with reservations* — teaching that "I'm partly sure, and here's why" is a strong conclusion, not a weak one.

## 2. The Six Sources (the case file — fictionalized realistically around the real 1835 story)
1. **A soldier's diary (1835)** — primary; written days after Gonzales; describes burying "the little cannon" later on the march. Strong, but one witness.
2. **A newspaper story (1897)** — secondary, 60 years after; colorful, contradicts the diary on details; written to boost a town festival (motive!).
3. **The museum donor's family legend** — "Grandpa always said…" — testimony with love in it; weakest alone, worth checking against the rest.
4. **An 1836 army supply list** — primary document; lists a six-pound cannon of matching size sent toward Gonzales. Dry, dull, and gold.
5. **A modern archaeologist's report (2010s)** — secondary but expert; metal analysis dates the barrel to the right era; can't prove *which* gun.
6. **A rival town's website** — claims *their* cannon is the real one; loaded words, no citations — the bias-spotting showcase.

## 3. Mechanics
Meters: **Evidence** 🔍 (what's solidly established), **Credibility** ⚖️ (how well the student weighed sources), **Case** 📋 (the argument taking shape). Sample decisions — *Phase 1, decision 1:* the diary is: ✅ primary — made at the time by a witness (Evidence +10) · ⚠️ secondary because it's old (feedback fixes the misconception: age ≠ secondary) · ❌ unusable because it's handwritten. *Decision 2:* one witness says it — ✅ log it and seek corroboration (Credibility +10) · ❌ case closed, the diary said so (Credibility −10; *"one source is a lead, not a verdict"*). The 1897 newspaper phase teaches motive; the website phase teaches loaded language (click the three loaded words — a mini-interaction); the finale grades the claim: ✅ "probably the gun — diary + supply list + metal date agree; the legend and the rival claim don't hold up" (Case +15).

Ending = sum → tiers ("Master Sleuth" / "Solid Case" / "Reopened for Review") + debrief: the real Gonzales cannon's fate is genuinely debated — which is the point: history is detective work that stays honest about uncertainty.

## 4. Assets & Command Center
Higgsfield: title card (a museum workroom, an old cannon on a bench, files and a magnifier), six source-card illustrations (a diary page, a yellowed newspaper, a family photo, a ledger, a lab report, a garish webpage mock — Sonnet can style #6). One class-wide group; PDF footer TEKS 7.20A/D/F. Pairs with the Primary or Secondary? and Spot the Bias apps as the graded capstone.
